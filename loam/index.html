Overview

A two-sided marketplace, designed end to end

Loam is a mission-driven marketplace for sustainable, organic products. I led design across both sides of the marketplace: the shopper experience and the seller tools. The work spanned UX architecture, a scalable design system, and the brand identity that ties it together.

The product brings vetted brands together across Wardrobe, Beauty & Wellness, Living, and Children. It gives shoppers one trusted place to discover better-for-you alternatives. The harder problem sat underneath the storefront. I had to design the systems that let independent vendors join, list, and operate, while keeping the experience consistent and trustworthy as the catalog scales.

Research

Understanding conscious shoppers

I studied how people discover and decide on sustainable products: what builds trust, what creates doubt, and where conventional marketplaces fall short for values-driven shoppers.

These signals shaped an experience that leads with transparency and curation rather than endless choice. The goal was to help shoppers feel confident that everything on Loam is genuinely good for them and the Earth.

Design System

Structuring the marketplace before designing it

Before any visual design, I mapped the full marketplace: every page, how shoppers and sellers move through it, and how data flows between them. Then I broke each page into reusable components, each one tagged by how its content is managed.

A content model for scale

The hard problem was keeping a large, multi-vendor catalog coherent without a large team to maintain it. I designed a content model with three zone types. Evergreen zones hold static brand and structure. Rotational zones update automatically from product and recommendation feeds. Manual zones hold the editorial and merchandising work that needs a person. Most of the site stays current on its own, so human effort goes only where it adds the most value.

Applying the model to every page

With the content model defined, I applied it to every page type. The homepage, category, product, corporate, and journal templates are all assembled from the same evergreen, rotational, and manual zones. Blocking each layout this way kept the system consistent before any visual design began.

Breaking pages into components

Each template then decomposes into a component inventory, and every component maps to one of the three content zones. That mapping is what lets most of the catalog stay current on its own.

The three zones split the work cleanly. Evergreen holds the static brand and structural UI. Rotational updates automatically from product and recommendation feeds. Manual covers the editorial pieces a person still owns: hero messaging, curated stories, and custom promotions. Across page types the mix skews evergreen and rotational, so the catalog largely maintains itself.

Prototyping the core flows

With the structure in place, I moved into mid-fidelity to pressure-test the highest-stakes moments on both sides of the marketplace. I prototyped checkout and order confirmation for shoppers, and order history and the seller login for vendors. Working through these flows surfaced the states and edge cases the component system would need to support.

A scalable component system

40+ reusable components, one coherent brand

I built a scalable design system of 40+ reusable components to ensure consistency across product and marketing touchpoints. Every surface, from storefront to seller portal to campaign, reads as one coherent brand.

Brand identity

The identity is warm, aspirational, and values-driven, expressed through "For Earth, With Love." Typography, color, and imagery work together to feel premium and editorial while staying approachable. The result reinforces trust without ever feeling clinical.

Solution

Designing the seller and shopper journeys

I designed the core workflows across both sides of the marketplace. This covered vendor onboarding, the seller portal, product listing, shipping and returns, and the shopper journey from discovery to checkout. I also designed the category system, which spans four departments and roughly 25 subcategories.

Loam runs on Shopify Collective, which provides the underlying multi-seller infrastructure for payments, vendor payouts, and order routing. My work was the experience layer on top. I designed how vendors move through onboarding and manage their storefronts. I also designed how shoppers discover and trust products across 300+ vendors without the experience feeling fragmented. I designed and built most of the marketplace myself, with two senior engineering consultants supporting a few focused tasks. Designing within Collective's constraints, rather than building infrastructure from scratch, kept the marketplace shippable on a small team.

Constraints and decisions

The recurring tradeoff was curation versus scale. The brand depends on everything feeling vetted and editorial. A multi-seller marketplace pulls toward volume and inconsistency. I prioritized a tightly designed system of components, templates, and guardrails. This let the catalog grow without each new vendor diluting the experience.

Outcomes

A coherent marketplace, built to scale

Loam is live as a unified marketplace supporting 300+ vendors, selling products from independent brands that ship direct from the maker. A design system and brand identity keep the experience consistent as the catalog grows. The full supporting system is live too: seller onboarding, product management, order tracking, and a returns flow that connects shoppers directly with each brand.

Reflection

Takeaways

Designing within a platform's limits

Building on Shopify Collective meant designing inside fixed boundaries rather than from a blank canvas. I learned to treat those limits as a starting point, shaping a branded, lower-friction experience on top of infrastructure I did not control.

Systems over screens

Keeping a 300+ vendor catalog coherent came down to the system, not individual pages. A content model of evergreen, rotational, and manual zones let most of the site stay current on its own, so a small team could scale without losing consistency.

Balancing curation and scale

The constant tension was editorial warmth versus marketplace volume. I leaned on tightly designed components, templates, and guardrails so the catalog could grow without each new vendor diluting the brand.
